Partner Portfolio — Coverage & Segmentation

How many partners we have, how they split by segment, turnover (GMV) and profitability, how much the team currently covers — and why there is no capacity to take on new partners, especially in SMB and Mid-market (MM).

For: team · director · sales director Partners: 487Locations: 4 140 GMV (Jan–May 2026): €1 346 135Generated 24 Jun 2026

Executive summary (30 sec)

  • Portfolio: 487 partners (205 active with GMV) / 4 140 locations / €1 346 135 GMV over Jan–May 2026. Steep pyramid: Enterprise + MM = 17.1% of active partners but 84.7% of GMV.
  • SMB is a long tail: 170 active partners (82.9%) generate just 15.3% of GMV. Average SMB partner = €1 208 GMV vs €55 747 for Enterprise — a ~46× gap.
  • The team actively manages 39 key partners with only 3 account managers (AM) — covering the large majority of portfolio GMV.
  • Load is critically skewed: one AM (M. Brynchak) carries 1 267 locations and ~68.6% of portfolio GMV. Meanwhile 265 locations across 193 partners have no AM assigned.
  • Conclusion: there is no capacity to onboard more managed partners. Every new SMB/MM partner consumes the same effort as Enterprise but returns a fraction of the value.

3. Segment economics: value vs effort

Managing one partner costs roughly the same effort regardless of segment — but the return differs by an order of magnitude.

How to read it

  • For the same managing effort, an Enterprise partner delivers €55 747 of GMV; an SMB partner delivers only €1 208.
  • SMB is 82.9% of active partners but just 15.3% of GMV. As a tail it can only be served at scale (self-serve / automation), not one-by-one by an AM.
  • Note on profitability: SMB runs a positive CP L1 (€25 741) on high commission rates, while Enterprise/MM are intentionally subsidised (campaigns & incentives) and run negative CP L1 to drive volume. So the constraint is not SMB profitability per euro — it is that each SMB partner is simply too small to justify hands-on management.

How to read it

  • A dedicated AM cuts churn ~8.2×: 2.6% with an AM vs 21.4% without. Hands-on coverage clearly keeps partners alive.
  • The tail bleeds fastest exactly where we have no capacity: SMB with no AM churns 22.0%, while managed SMB stays at 4.2%. Enterprise is stickier but still churns 18.2% without an AM.
  • Implication: the existing book genuinely needs retention attention, but the 3 AMs are already full — so the answer is added capacity / self-serve for the tail, not piling more partners onto 3 people.

Churn = location active in Jan–Feb 2026 with no delivered orders in Apr–May 2026. Some managed/no-AM cells have small cohorts (n shown) — read those as directional.

How each metric is calculated

  • Unit = location (an individual store / provider_id), not the partner-chain. Source: Databricks fact_order_delivery × dim_provider_v2 — Ukraine, store vertical, delivered orders, 2026. Monthly delivered-order counts per location (Jan…May).
  • Active cohort = locations that had delivered orders in January OR February (i.e. were "alive" at the start of the year). Two months are used to smooth single-month noise.
  • Churned = a location in the cohort that had no delivered orders in either April or May (went silent by the end of the period).
  • Churn % = churned ÷ cohort.
  • Churn (all) = churn over every location in the segment.
  • Churn — managed (n) = churn only among locations whose partner has one of the 3 dedicated AMs; (n) = that sub-cohort's size.
  • Churn — no AM (n) = churn among locations with no dedicated AM; (n) = that sub-cohort's size. Note: managed n + no-AM n = Active cohort.
  • "Churn (all)" is a weighted figure, not the average of the two sub-columns — it leans toward whichever group has more locations (e.g. Enterprise toward managed, SMB toward no-AM).

Example — Enterprise: 532 active in Jan–Feb; 14 churned → 2.6%. Of those, 521 are managed (12 churned → 2.3%) and 11 have no AM (2 churned → 18.2%); 12 + 2 = 14.

8. Conclusion: why there is no capacity for new partners

Argument

  • 1. Only 3 AMs for the whole key portfolio. They already cover 2 904 locations (33 live partners + 6 future chains). The team is physically at its limit.
  • 2. Concentration risk. One AM (M. Brynchak) holds ~68.6% of GMV and 1 267 locations. Adding partners without adding people deepens the imbalance.
  • 3. Economics against SMB/MM. A new SMB partner brings on average just €1 208 of GMV yet needs the same onboarding and ongoing support as an Enterprise partner at €55 747 — a ~46× worse effort-to-value ratio.
  • 4. Focus on value. 265 locations across 193 partners already have no AM. We are not even fully covering the existing book; spreading thinner onto small SMB/MM would weaken work with top partners.
  • 5. The tail does not scale by hand. 170 active SMB partners cannot be managed individually with the current team — that is a self-serve / automation job, not an AM job.
  • 6. Coverage is what keeps partners alive. Partners without a dedicated AM churn ~8.2× more (21.4% vs 2.6%). The unmanaged tail is already bleeding, yet the 3 AMs cannot absorb it — new partners would either get no support (and churn) or pull attention away from the accounts that actually drive GMV.

Recommended instead of "take more"

  • Freeze intake of new SMB/MM into hands-on management until additional headcount is added.
  • First cover (or move to self-serve) the 265 locations that currently have no AM.
  • Rebalance the book to remove the ~68.6% GMV single-AM concentration risk.
  • Onboard new partners only selectively in Enterprise / high-MM above segment-average GMV.
  • Serve the SMB tail through automated / group processes, not individual management.
