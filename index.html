How to read it

How each metric is calculated

  • Unit = location (an individual store / provider_id), not the partner-chain. Source: Databricks fact_order_delivery × dim_provider_v2 — Ukraine, store vertical, delivered orders, 2026. Monthly delivered-order counts per location (Jan…May).
  • Active cohort = locations that had delivered orders in January OR February (i.e. were "alive" at the start of the year). Two months are used to smooth single-month noise.
  • Churned = a location in the cohort that had no delivered orders in either April or May (went silent by the end of the period).
  • Churn % = churned ÷ cohort.
  • Churn (all) = churn over every location in the segment.
  • Churn — managed (n) = churn only among locations whose partner has one of the 3 dedicated AMs; (n) = that sub-cohort's size.
  • Churn — no AM (n) = churn among locations with no dedicated AM; (n) = that sub-cohort's size. Note: managed n + no-AM n = Active cohort.
  • "Churn (all)" is a weighted figure, not the average of the two sub-columns — it leans toward whichever group has more locations (e.g. Enterprise toward managed, SMB toward no-AM).
  • Partner-level churn applies the same rule to the whole chain (group): a partner is in the cohort if any of its stores was active in Jan–Feb, and churns only if all its stores went silent by May–Jun. Big multi-store chains almost never fully churn, so this is naturally lower than location churn.
  • GMV lost = the actual Jan–Jun GMV generated by churned locations (revenue that existed and has now stopped). Run-rate / mo = those same locations' average monthly GMV while active (Jan–Feb), i.e. the recurring revenue no longer coming in. % of seg GMV = GMV lost ÷ that segment's total GMV.

Example — Enterprise: 689 active in Jan–Feb; 38 churned → 5.5%. Of those, 657 are managed (31 churned → 4.7%) and 32 have no AM (7 churned → 21.9%).

7. SMB → outsource to Cognizant: the case, forecast & ROI

The SMB tail is too large and too low-value to manage with in-house AMs, yet it churns badly without help. Outsourcing it to Cognizant (at €1,000/agent/month) covers the tail cheaply, stops the leakage, and frees the 3 in-house AMs for high-value Enterprise/MM.

Why SMB should be outsourced to Cognizant

  • 1. Volume the in-house team can't reach. 174 active SMB partners and ~117 new SMB partners onboarded per year — while 3 AMs are already maxed on Enterprise/MM. The SMB tail is structurally unmanageable in-house.
  • 2. It leaks without coverage. Of ~117 SMB partners onboarded in the last 12 months, only +21 stuck — about 82% leaked away. SMB locations with no AM churn 24.5% vs 0% when managed.
  • 3. The unit economics only work outsourced. An SMB partner is worth ~€3 401 GMV/year — far too little to justify a full in-house AM, but easily profitable for a €1,000/month agent covering ~120 partners.
  • 4. SMB is profitable at contribution level (23.5% commission, positive CP L1) — it is worth keeping, just not at in-house cost.
  • 5. It frees our 3 AMs for the real growth. Enterprise GMV is scaling fast (≈4× in 12 months); every hour spent on a €3,401-a-year SMB partner is an hour not spent there.
  • 6. SMB management is standardised & repeatable (onboarding, menu/photo coverage, availability, promo nudges, basic QBRs) — an ideal playbook to hand to an outsourcing partner.

9. Conclusion: why there is no capacity for new partners

Argument

  • 1. Only 3 AMs for the whole key portfolio. They already cover 1 861 locations (34 live partners + 7 future chains). The team is physically at its limit.
  • 2. Concentration risk. One AM (M. Brynchak) holds ~60.6% of GMV and 1 247 locations. Adding partners without adding people deepens the imbalance.
  • 3. Economics against SMB/MM. A new SMB partner brings on average just €1 240 of GMV yet needs the same onboarding and ongoing support as an Enterprise partner at €62 695 — a ~51× worse effort-to-value ratio.
  • 4. Focus on value. 1 129 locations across 186 partners already have no AM. We are not even fully covering the existing book; spreading thinner onto small SMB/MM would weaken work with top partners.
  • 5. The tail does not scale by hand. 174 active SMB partners cannot be managed individually with the current team — that is a self-serve / automation job, not an AM job.
  • 6. Coverage is what keeps partners alive. Partners without a dedicated AM churn ~4.8× more (22.2% vs 4.6%). The unmanaged tail is already bleeding, yet the 3 AMs cannot absorb it — new partners would either get no support (and churn) or pull attention away from the accounts that actually drive GMV.

Recommended instead of "take more"

  • Freeze intake of new SMB/MM into hands-on management until additional headcount is added.
  • First cover (or move to self-serve) the 1 129 locations that currently have no AM.
  • Rebalance the book to remove the ~60.6% GMV single-AM concentration risk.
  • Onboard new partners only selectively in Enterprise / high-MM above segment-average GMV.
  • Outsource the SMB tail to Cognizant (~€1,000/agent/month): it stops the 82% onboarding leak, cuts churn from 24.5% to ~5.0%, and pays back in ~5.3 months (ROI ≈ 127%) — while freeing the 3 in-house AMs for Enterprise/MM.
